DEFORMATION, STRESS, AND STRAIN

Within the Earth, rocks are continually being subjected to forces that tend to bend, twist, or fracture them. When rocks bend, twist or fracture we say that they deform. The forces that cause deformation of rock are referred to as stresses.

Stress is the force per unit area that is placed on a rock. Strain is a change in shape or size resulting from applied forces (deformation per unit area). Rocks only strain when placed under stress.

TYPES OF STRESS AND STRAIN

UNIFORM STRESS

The force applies equally on all sides of a body of rock. Also known as pressure. Rocks in the Earth all experience uniform stress at all times. It is also called lithostatic pressure, which comes from the weight of rocks at a certain point of the Earth. When there is an ocean on top of the rock, it is called hydrostatic pressure.

DIFFERENTIAL STRESS

Stress is not equal from all directions of a body of a rock. Tension is a directed stress that pulls rock apart in opposite directions through stretching out. Compression is a stress that pushes rocks together. Shear is a directed stress that pushes one side of a body of rock in one direction, and the opposite side of the body of rock in the opposite direction.

ELASTIC STRAIN

It is reversible. Rock that has undergone only elastic strain will go back to its original shape if the stress is released.

DUCTILE STRAIN

It is irreversible. A rock that has undergone ductile strain will remain deformed even if the stress stops. Another term for ductile strain is plastic deformation.

FRACTURE

It is also called rupture. A rock that has ruptured has abruptly broken into distinct pieces. If the pieces are offset—shifted in opposite directions from each other—the fracture is a fault.

FACTORS THAT AFFECT THE BEHAVIOR OF MATERIALS

BRITTLE MATERIALS

They have a small or large region of elastic behavior but only a small region of ductile behavior before they fracture. They respond by breaking or fracturing.

DUCTILE MATERIALS

They have a small region of elastic behavior and a large region of ductile behavior before they fracture. They respond by bending without breaking apart.

TEMPERATURE

At high temperature molecules and their bonds can stretch and move, thus materials will behave in more ductile manner. At low temperature, materials are brittle.

CONFINING PRESSURE

Defined as the pressure or stress imposed on a layer of soil or rock by the weight of overlying material. At high confining pressure, materials are less likely to fracture compared to at low confining stress where material will be brittle and will tend to fracture sooner.

STRAIN RATE

At high strain rates, material tends to fracture. At low strain rates more time is available for individual atoms to move and therefore ductile behavior is favored.

COMPOSITION

Some minerals, like quartz, olivine, and feldspars are very brittle. Others, like clay minerals, micas, and calcite are more ductile. This is due to the chemical bond types that hold them together. Water appears to weaken the chemical bonds and forms films around mineral grains along which slippage can take place. Thus wet rock tends to behave in ductile manner, while dry rocks tend to behave in brittle manner.

EFFECTS OF DEFORMATION (DEPENDING ON TYPE OF STRESS & MATERIAL)

JOINTS

Natural cracks on the surface produced by brittle deformation due to tensional stress.

FOLDS

Contortion of rock layers forming wave-like curves due to ductile deformation caused by compressional stress.

FAULTS

Planar structure resulting from brittle deformation of sliding rocks.

METAMORPHISM AND ITS FACTORS

METAMORPHISM

It is the physical and/or chemical alteration of minerals or geological composition in pre-existing rocks, without the protolith (the original rock) melting into liquid magma or disintegrating. The transition occurs mainly due to heat, pressure, and addition of chemically active fluids.

FACTORS OF
METAMORPHISM

(1) the mineral composition of the parent rock. (the original rock from which something else was formed), (2) the temperature at which metamorphism takes place, (3) the amount and type of pressure during metamorphism, and (4) the content of fluids (mostly water) that are present during metamorphism.

TYPES OF METAMORPHISM

REGIONAL

Occurs due to pressure and heat applied to rocks by converging plates and mountain building. Produces a large volume of metamorphic rocks. High pressure and temperature but low water.

CONTACT

Occurs due to heating (baking) of country rocks by rising magma. High temperature, no added pressure. Metamorphism is confined to the region surrounding the intrusion, called the contact or metamorphic aureole.

HYDROTHERMAL

Driven by hot fluid, commonly water. The addition of fluids promotes recrystallization and neocrystallization. Occurs in oceanic ridges, subduction zones, and groundwater near magma chambers.

CATACLASTIC (DYNAMIC)

Happens due to the shearing of plastic-like rocks near the fault lines. The friction of sliding creates heat and due to shearing the rocks appear to be mechanically deformed, crushed and pulverized.

BURIAL

Happens due to the confining pressure of overlying rocks and the increasing temperature and pressures at the deeper portions of the crust.

SHOCK

Happens due to the pressure brought by the impact of extraterrestrial objects (i.e. meteorites); metamorphic rocks formed from this process are called “impactite.”
