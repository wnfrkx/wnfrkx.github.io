Four Layers of the Earth



Earth's Internal Heat

Primordial Heat
When the planet Earth is formed and shaped from meteorites that smashed with each other until it reached its current size, that process (planetary accretion) gained a lot of heat and became a source of Earth’s heat. The movement/transportation of heavy metals due to gravity which leads to frictional heating is also one of the sources of Earth’s heat.

Nuclear Decay
Nuclear decay represents about 50% of earth’s heat. Unstable or radioactive elements undergo spontaneous fission into other isotopes and elements to become stable, releasing energy in the process.

Movement of Earth's Heat

Conduction
Heat energy is transferred between adjacent atoms or molecules by collisions.

Convection
The movement of a hot fluid, such as air or water, moves heat.

Radiation
Does not rely on any interaction between the source of heat and the heated material, but rather on waves across space.

Magmatism

Magmatism

Magma is molten or partly molten rock from which igneous rocks form, with temperatures up to 1400°C. It normally consists of silicate liquid, while melts of carbonate and sulfide also exist. Gasses such as carbon dioxide and water vapor are also present. It is capable of rising to the surface through pushing through cracks and holes in the crust, hence volcanic eruptions.

Viscosity is the resistance to flow that depends on the temperature and silica content. Lower temperature + high silica content = more viscous and vice versa. It can also affect the shape of the volcano. Volcanic eruptions can be effusive or explosive, depending on the viscosity.

Three variables that primarily influence the formation of magma can be summarized: Temperature, pressure, and composition.

Processes that affect Magma Formation



Partial Melting
Since the mantle consists of several different minerals, it does not uniformly melt. Those with higher melting points continue as solid crystals, as minerals with lower melting points transform into molten magma. The rock melting temperature, called the eutectic temperature, is lower than the constituent mineral melting temperature.

Fractional Crystallization
It raises the magma silica content, making it more felsic. As the temperature drops through the crust inside a magma diapir, some minerals will crystallize and settle to the bottom of the magma chamber, leaving certain ions drained from the remaining melt.

Types of Magma Formation

Heat Transfer Melting

Rocks melt from heat transfer from rising magma. The mantle’s rising magma brings heat which can melt surrounding rocks at shallower depths. This is present in rift valleys, hotspots, subduction zones and oceanic ridges.

Decompression Melting

It requires the upward displacement of the earth's mantle to a region of lower pressure. The reduction in overlying pressure causes the rock to melt, contributing to magma formation. This is present in rift valleys, hotspots, and oceanic ridges.

Flux Melting

It happens when rock is added to water, carbon dioxide, or volatile gasses. These compounds cause the rock to melt at lower temperatures. This induces magma in areas where it originally maintained a solid structure. This is present in subduction zones.
